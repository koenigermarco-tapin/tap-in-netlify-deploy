The Two Types of Trust

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • The critical difference between predictability-based trust and vulnerability-based trust
  • Why high-performing teams require vulnerability-based trust
  • How to assess which type of trust exists in your current team

üìñ Core Concept

Most people confuse reliability with trust. They think: "I trust Sarah because she always delivers on time." That's predictability-based trust‚Äîuseful, but limited. It means you can predict behavior. It doesn't mean you can be vulnerable.

Vulnerability-based trust is different. According to Lencioni's research, it's the confidence that your teammates will not use your weaknesses against you. It's knowing you can say "I don't know," "I was wrong," or "I need help" without being punished, judged, or secretly tallied against.

Amy Edmondson's research at Harvard defines this as psychological safety: "a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking."

üìä Research Evidence: Google's Project Aristotle

Google's 2-year study of 180 teams found that psychological safety‚Äîthe belief that you won't be punished when you make a mistake‚Äîis the #1 predictor of team performance.

  • 43% of variance in team performance correlated with psychological safety
  • 19% higher productivity with fewer errors
  • 27% lower turnover rates
  • 3.6x more engagement and job satisfaction

üîç Why This Matters

Teams without vulnerability-based trust operate in constant self-protection mode. People hedge their language, hide their mistakes, and avoid asking for help. Energy that should go toward results gets wasted on political maneuvering and impression management.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Predictability-based trust lets you rely on someone's behavior; vulnerability-based trust lets you be honest with them
  • Teams without vulnerability-based trust waste enormous energy on self-protection
  • Building vulnerability-based trust requires someone to go first‚Äîusually the leader

Why Teams Avoid Vulnerability

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • The evolutionary and professional reasons we avoid vulnerability
  • How organizational culture punishes vulnerability
  • The cost of maintaining a "professional mask"

üìñ Core Concept

Vulnerability feels dangerous because, for most of human history, it was. Being excluded from the tribe meant death. Our brains evolved to prioritize social belonging above almost everything else.

Modern workplaces amplify this fear. Promotions go to people who "have it all together." Performance reviews punish mistakes. Compensation systems reward individual achievement over collective success.

The result: professionals spend enormous energy managing how they're perceived. They craft careful responses. They hide errors. They pretend to have answers they don't have. All of this is exhausting and counterproductive.

üìä Research Evidence: The Cost of Hiding

Studies on impression management show that employees spend an average of 2.3 hours per day managing how they're perceived by others‚Äîenergy that could go toward actual work.

Teams with high psychological safety experience 27% fewer errors because people report mistakes immediately rather than hiding them.

In Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu, you can't progress without getting comfortable being vulnerable. You'll be pinned, submitted, and completely dominated‚Äîespecially as a beginner.

The people who progress fastest? Those who tap early, ask questions, and admit when they don't understand a technique. The ones who progress slowly? Those who pretend they've got it and never ask for help.

The Trust Pyramid

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • The four levels of team trust
  • How to diagnose where your team currently sits
  • The progression path from transactional to transformational trust

üìñ The Four Levels

Level 1: Transactional Trust ‚Äî "I trust you'll do your job." This is the baseline. People show up, do their tasks, and meet deadlines. But there's no depth, no real connection.

Level 2: Competency Trust ‚Äî "I trust your skills and judgment." People respect each other's expertise. They defer to subject matter experts. But vulnerability still isn't safe.

Level 3: Relational Trust ‚Äî "I trust you care about me as a person." People know each other beyond work. There's genuine concern for well-being. But difficult truths still aren't discussed.

Level 4: Vulnerability-Based Trust ‚Äî "I trust I can be fully honest with you‚Äîabout mistakes, fears, and doubts‚Äîwithout penalty." This is where high performance lives.

üìä Research Evidence: Trust and Performance

Paul Zak's research on organizational trust found that employees in high-trust companies:

  • 74% less stress
  • 50% higher productivity
  • 76% more engagement
  • 40% less burnout

But most companies operate at Level 1 or 2.

Signs of Trust Dysfunction

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • Observable behaviors that indicate low trust
  • How to recognize when your team is in trust crisis
  • The cascading effects of trust breakdown

üìñ Warning Signs

1. Meetings after meetings: The real conversation happens in parking lots and Slack DMs, not in the official meeting. This signals people don't trust the group with their real opinions.

2. Guarded language: Everything is hedged. "I think maybe we might consider possibly..." People use language designed to minimize personal risk.

3. CYA behavior: People document everything, copy everyone on emails, and create paper trails. They're building evidence in case something goes wrong.

4. No one admits mistakes: Errors are hidden, minimized, or blamed on external factors. "The data was wrong" rather than "I misread the data."

5. Help isn't asked for: People struggle alone rather than ask teammates for support. Asking for help feels like admitting incompetence.

üìä The 2025 Talent Trends Austria Report

According to recent research on workplace trust in Austria:

  • Only 1% of employees say they "fully trust leadership"
  • 31% say they "somewhat trust" leadership
  • 68% report low or no trust in organizational leadership

This isn't a personnel problem. It's a systemic trust crisis.

ü•ã The BJJ Mirror

Low-trust teams are like training partners who won't tap. They let the submission get tighter and tighter rather than admit they're caught. Eventually, something tears.

In healthy training rooms, tapping is normalized. You tap, you learn, you improve. In dysfunctional rooms, tapping is stigmatized. People get injured.
