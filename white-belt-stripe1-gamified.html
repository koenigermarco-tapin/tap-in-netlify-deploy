Lesson 1
The Two Types of Trust
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • The critical difference between predictability-based trust and vulnerability-based trust
  • Why high-performing teams require vulnerability-based trust
  • How to assess which type of trust exists in your current team

üìñ Core Concept

Most people confuse reliability with trust. They think: "I trust Sarah because she always delivers on time." That's predictability-based trust‚Äîuseful, but limited. It means you can predict behavior. It doesn't mean you can be vulnerable.

Vulnerability-based trust is different. According to Lencioni's research, it's the confidence that your teammates will not use your weaknesses against you. It's knowing you can say "I don't know," "I was wrong," or "I need help" without being punished, judged, or secretly tallied against.

Amy Edmondson's research at Harvard defines this as psychological safety: "a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking."

üìä Research Evidence: Google's Project Aristotle

Google's 2-year study of 180 teams found that psychological safety‚Äîthe belief that you won't be punished when you make a mistake‚Äîis the #1 predictor of team performance.

  • 43% of variance in team performance correlated with psychological safety
  • 19% higher productivity with fewer errors
  • 27% lower turnover rates
  • 3.6x more engagement and job satisfaction

üîç Why This Matters

Teams without vulnerability-based trust operate in constant self-protection mode. People hedge their language, hide their mistakes, and avoid asking for help. Energy that should go toward results gets wasted on political maneuvering and impression management.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Predictability-based trust lets you rely on someone's behavior; vulnerability-based trust lets you be honest with them
  • Teams without vulnerability-based trust waste enormous energy on self-protection
  • Building vulnerability-based trust requires someone to go first‚Äîusually the leader

üìñ Core Concept

If vulnerability-based trust is so powerful, why don't more teams have it?

Because vulnerability feels dangerous‚Äîand for most people, it has been.

üìä The Trust Crisis: Talent Trends 2025 Austria Report

  • Only 1% of employees fully trust leadership to balance performance and wellbeing
  • Just 31% say their company fosters psychological safety
  • Only 32% feel leaders lead by example
  • 35% believe leadership consistently acts in line with company values

üß† The Competence Trap

Most professionals have been trained by experience to protect themselves. They've learned that showing weakness is career suicide. Even if their current organization claims to value transparency, their nervous system remembers every time honesty backfired.

High achievers often build their identity around being the person with answers. Admitting "I don't know" feels like an identity threat. It's easier to fake confidence than to risk being seen as incompetent.

ü•ã The BJJ Analogy

In Brazilian Jiu Jitsu, you can't fake competence. The mat reveals everything. A blue belt who pretends to be a black belt will be exposed in seconds. This forced honesty is why martial arts communities develop trust so quickly‚Äîthere's nowhere to hide.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Past punishment for honesty trains people to self-protect
  • High achievers often struggle with vulnerability because their identity is built on competence
  • Stated values don't create trust‚Äîdemonstrated behaviors do

üîó The Cascade Effect

Without trust ‚Üí No real conflict. People won't disagree honestly if they fear vulnerability will be weaponized. You get artificial harmony‚Äîeveryone nodding in meetings, then complaining in private.

Without conflict ‚Üí No real commitment. If concerns aren't voiced, decisions feel imposed rather than chosen. People comply instead of commit.

Without commitment ‚Üí No real accountability. Why would anyone hold a colleague accountable to a decision they never truly supported?

Without accountability ‚Üí No real results. Individual goals trump team goals. People optimize for personal success, not collective outcomes.

üìä McKinsey Research: The Cost of Disengagement

Employee disengagement and attrition could cost a median-size S&P 500 company between $228 million and $355 million a year in lost productivity. Over five years, that's at least $1.1 billion in lost value per company.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Trust is foundational‚Äîyou cannot skip it and address "higher" dysfunctions
  • Artificial harmony (fake agreement) is a symptom of missing trust
  • Accountability systems fail without trust as the foundation

üìñ Observable Signs of Trust Dysfunction

Trust dysfunction often hides behind other explanations. Leaders say "we have a communication problem" or "people aren't aligned on priorities." Usually, the real problem is trust.

Sign What It Looks Like
Meetings after meetings The real conversations happen in hallways, DMs, and 1-on-1s‚Äînever in the room where decisions should be made
Careful language People hedge, qualify, and caveat everything. "I might be wrong, but..." "This is just my opinion, however..."
Reluctance to ask for help People would rather struggle alone than admit they're stuck. Projects slow down because no one says "I need support"
Blame and credit games Failures get attributed to others; successes get claimed individually. No one says "we" when things go wrong
Invisible effort People work hard to appear competent rather than to be effective. Presentations are polished, but underlying problems are hidden
High turnover with vague reasons Exit interviews cite "better opportunities" or "career growth," but the real reason is exhaustion from constant self-protection

üìä Deloitte Research: The Cost of Turnover

Every time a business replaces a salaried employee, it costs six to nine months of their average salary. For an employee making ‚Ç¨60,000 a year, that's ‚Ç¨30,000 to ‚Ç¨45,000 in recruiting and training expenses.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Trust dysfunction often masquerades as "communication problems" or "misalignment"
  • Observable behaviors (hedging, side conversations, blame games) reveal trust levels
  • Performance without trust is unsustainable‚Äîit leads to burnout and attrition
