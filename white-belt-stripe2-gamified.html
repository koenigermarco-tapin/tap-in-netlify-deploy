The Vulnerability First Move

üéØ What You'll Learn

üìñ Core Concept

In BJJ, there's a concept called "giving your back." It's one of the worst positions‚Äîyour opponent can choke you, and you can't see what's coming. No one voluntarily gives their back in competition.

Except in training with trusted partners. When you give your back intentionally, you're saying: "I trust you not to destroy me. Let's work on escape techniques."

This is the vulnerability first move. Someone has to go first, and in teams, it must be the leader.

üìä Paul J. Zak's Research

Leaders asking for help increases trust and cooperation throughout the organization. "Showing vulnerability" is one of eight key management behaviors that stimulate oxytocin production and generate trust.

üéØ Strategic Vulnerability Examples

  • Admit knowledge gaps: "I don't understand the technical details here‚Äîcan someone walk me through it?"
  • Share past failures: "I made this exact mistake before. Here's what I learned."
  • Ask for feedback: "What's one thing I could do better in these meetings?"
  • Acknowledge emotions: "I'm frustrated by this situation, and I'm working through it."

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Distinction

Strategic vulnerability means exposing real limitations, not curated weaknesses. People can smell performative vulnerability instantly. It destroys trust faster than silence.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Leaders must go first‚Äîwaiting for your team to be vulnerable is a losing strategy
  • Strategic vulnerability means exposing real limitations, not curated weaknesses
  • Performative vulnerability (fake admissions) destroys trust faster than silence

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • How sharing personal context humanizes colleagues

üìñ Lencioni's Personal Histories Exercise

This is one of the fastest trust-building interventions available, adapted for Tap-In's embodied approach.

The exercise is simple: Each team member answers 3-5 questions about their background. Not work history‚Äîpersonal history.

üí¨ Recommended Questions

  • Where did you grow up? How many siblings?
  • What was your biggest challenge in childhood?
  • What was your first job (ever, not career-related)?
  • What's a unique or interesting fact about you that others might not know?

üß† Why This Works

Most professionals know their colleagues only as functional roles. "Sarah the engineer." "Mike from finance." When you learn someone's context‚Äîtheir background, struggles, and formative experiences‚Äîyou see them as a full human being. Judgment decreases. Empathy increases. Trust follows.

üìä Disclosure vs. Vulnerability

Sharing that you grew up in a small town is disclosure. Sharing that you felt out of place when you arrived at university because everyone seemed more prepared‚Äîthat's vulnerability. The exercise works best when people share context that felt emotionally significant.

ü•ã Tap-In Enhancement

In Tap-In workshops, we combine this with physical warm-ups‚Äîlight partner exercises before the conversation. Research shows that 70-90% of communication is non-verbal, and physical contact (even just pushing hands palm-to-palm) accelerates trust because it bypasses the verbal brain.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Knowing personal context transforms colleagues from roles into humans
  • Emotional significance matters more than dramatic stories
  • Combining verbal sharing with physical exercises accelerates trust

‚ú® Practice Exercise

This week: Run a mini Personal History exercise with your team (or even just 2-3 colleagues). Use these questions:

  1. Where did you grow up?
  2. What was a defining challenge from your early life?
  3. What's something about your background that affects how you work today?

Keep it to 5 minutes per person. Notice how the room feels afterward.

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • Adapting embodied exercises for non-martial artists

üìñ The Grip-Switch Drill

This is Tap-In's signature trust-building drill, adapted from BJJ hand-fighting techniques for business teams.

ü•ã How to Perform the Drill

  1. Pair up. Face your partner, standing about arm's length apart.
  2. Connect. Both people extend their arms and touch palms, chest-high.
  3. Round 1 (30 seconds): Partner A tries to gently switch grip‚Äîsliding to grab Partner B's wrist. Partner B "gives way" (moves with the pressure instead of resisting), then mirrors the grip attempt.
  4. Switch roles and repeat.
  5. Round 2: Both partners try simultaneously, giving and taking in flow.
  6. Debrief (2 minutes): How did it feel to give way? To take? To flow?

üß† Why This Works

The drill creates controlled physical vulnerability without fighting or competition. You're touching someone, trying to control them, and also surrendering control. It's uncomfortable‚Äîwhich is exactly the point.

üìä Embodied Learning Research

Studies confirm that embodied learning outperforms cognitive-only approaches. The Strozzi Institute research shows that "reshaping of the brain (new synapses, myelination of neurons, neurogenesis) requires struggle" and embodied practice.

üí≠ Common Insights

When people reflect afterward, they often connect the physical experience to psychological patterns:

  • "I realized I always try to control instead of flowing."
  • "Giving way felt weak at first, then felt strategic."
  • "I don't trust easily‚ÄîI kept resisting even when I should have moved."

These insights translate directly to team dynamics.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Physical exercises create vulnerability faster than words alone
  • The Grip-Switch drill builds trust in 5-10 minutes with lasting effects
  • Physical experiences reveal psychological patterns (control, resistance, flow)

‚ú® Practice Exercise

Afterward, ask: "What did you notice about yourself during that exercise?" Listen to their answer before sharing yours.

üéØ What You'll Learn

üìñ Core Concept

Vulnerability is a skill. Like any skill, it improves with deliberate practice.

Here are three scenarios to practice‚Äîideally with a trusted colleague or coach who can roleplay the other side:

üé≠ Scenario 1: You don't understand something in a meeting

Typical Response Vulnerability Practice

üé≠ Scenario 2: You made a mistake that others might discover

Typical Response Vulnerability Practice

üé≠ Scenario 3: You're struggling with a task

Typical Response Vulnerability Practice

üéØ The Key Insight

Vulnerability isn't about exposing yourself randomly. It's about choosing to share what's real in moments when hiding would be easier. Each time you choose transparency over protection, you strengthen the trust muscle.

üìä Amy Edmondson's Research

Google's Project Aristotle, which built on Edmondson's work, identifies three key practices for cultivating psychological safety:

  • Frame work as a learning problem - Creates belief that everyone's input is needed
  • Acknowledge fallibility - Leaders say "I might miss something, I need to hear from you"
  • Model curiosity - Ask lots of questions to create need for voice and participation

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Vulnerability improves with deliberate scenario practice
  • Having practiced phrases reduces the cognitive load of being vulnerable in real moments
  • Each small act of transparency makes the next one easier
