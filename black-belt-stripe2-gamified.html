Lesson 1: Choosing the Right Metrics

The Metric Trap: Not all metrics are created equal. Teams often measure what's easy to track instead of what actually drives results, leading to misaligned effort and false progress.

The Vanity Metric Problem

Vanity metrics make you feel good but don't predict success or drive decisions. They're easy to measure, trend upward naturally, but don't correlate with business outcomes.

Classic Vanity Metrics:
  • Page views - Users might visit once and never return (retention matters more)
  • Total users - Including inactive users inflates success (active users matter)
  • Social media followers - Large audience with no engagement = no impact
  • Features shipped - More features ‚â† better product (customer value matters)
  • Email list size - 100K subscribers with 2% open rate < 5K with 40% open rate

Why teams love vanity metrics: They always go up (feels like progress), they're easy to report (no context needed), and they rarely force hard conversations about what's actually working.

Actionable Metrics: The Alternative

Actionable metrics change how you make decisions. They're tied to specific levers you can pull, predict future outcomes, and segment meaningfully.

Vanity Metric Actionable Alternative Why It's Better
Total users Daily Active Users (DAU) / Monthly Active Users (MAU) Shows engagement, not just acquisition
Page views Time to value (how fast users get benefit) Predicts retention better than traffic
Features shipped Feature adoption rate (% of users using new features) Measures value delivered, not activity
Revenue Customer Lifetime Value (LTV) / Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) Shows efficiency and sustainability
Support tickets closed First contact resolution rate + CSAT Measures quality, not just volume

The One Metric That Matters (OMTM)

At any given time, there's one metric that best represents success for your current stage. It's not the only metric you track, but it's the North Star that aligns everyone.

Startup Stage Examples:
  • Pre-Product/Market Fit: Weekly Active Users (are people using it consistently?)
  • Early Growth: Activation rate (% of signups who get to "aha moment")
  • Scaling: Net Revenue Retention (are customers staying and expanding?)
  • Mature: Customer Lifetime Value / CAC ratio (is growth profitable?)

How to identify your OMTM:

  1. What's the biggest constraint to growth right now?
  2. If you could only improve one thing this quarter, what would unlock the most value?
  3. What metric, if it doubled, would transform the business?

The Metrics Selection Framework

Use these filters to evaluate whether a metric deserves focus:

5 Questions to Validate a Metric:
  1. Actionable: Can you change this metric through specific actions?
  2. Comparable: Can you compare it across time periods, segments, or benchmarks?
  3. Understandable: Can everyone on the team explain what it means and why it matters?
  4. Ratio or Rate: Is it a ratio/rate (more insight) vs. absolute number (less context)?
  5. Behavior-Changing: Does tracking it influence how people work?

Rule: If a metric fails 3+ of these, it's probably not worth tracking.

Avoiding Metric Gaming

Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."

Classic Gaming Examples:
  • Call center time-per-call targets: Reps rush customers off phone, satisfaction drops
  • Sales team quota pressure: Reps close bad-fit customers who churn immediately
  • Code commit counts: Developers make trivial commits to inflate numbers
  • Ticket resolution time: Support closes tickets prematurely to hit SLA

Prevention strategies:

  • Pair metrics: Speed + quality (e.g., resolution time + CSAT)
  • Add guardrails: "Increase signups without NPS dropping below 40"
  • Spot-check quality: Random audit samples to catch gaming
  • Ask "how could this be gamed?" before rolling out incentives

üìö Research: Metrics and Performance

MIT Sloan study: Teams that focused on 1-3 key metrics outperformed teams tracking 10+ metrics by 35% in goal achievement. More metrics = diluted focus.

Harvard Business Review: 87% of companies track metrics that don't correlate with their stated strategic priorities. Common disconnect: activity metrics vs. outcome goals.

Key insight: The best teams ruthlessly cut metrics that don't drive decisions.

ü•ã The BJJ Parallel: Competition Stats

The difference? Vanity metrics make you feel busy. Actionable metrics show you where you're actually improving and where you're still vulnerable. One drives ego, the other drives growth.

üí™ Practice: Audit Your Metrics

Step 1: List the top 5 metrics your team currently tracks.

Step 2: For each metric, answer:

  • Is this a vanity metric or actionable metric?
  • Does improving this metric directly lead to success? Or is it a proxy?
  • Have you made a decision based on this metric in the last 30 days?
  • Could this metric be gamed? How?

Step 3: Identify your OMTM - the one metric that best represents success right now.

Step 4: Cut at least 2 metrics that don't pass the validation framework.

Lesson 2: Building Results Dashboards

Visibility Creates Accountability: When results are visible to everyone, constantly, the team naturally orients toward them. Dashboards aren't just reporting tools‚Äîthey're focusing mechanisms.

Why Most Dashboards Fail

The common approach: Throw every metric into a dashboard, make it look impressive, then never look at it again except during monthly reviews.

Dashboard Failure Patterns:
  • Too much data: 30 metrics on one screen = paralysis, not clarity
  • No hierarchy: Every metric looks equally important, so nothing is
  • Stale data: "Last updated 2 weeks ago" kills trust and urgency
  • Hidden away: Only accessible via login, buried in tools, not ambient
  • No context: Numbers without trends, benchmarks, or targets mean nothing

The result: Dashboards become wallpaper‚Äîtechnically visible but ignored in practice.

The Principles of Effective Results Dashboards

1. One Primary Metric, Prominently Displayed

Your OMTM (One Metric That Matters) should be impossible to miss. Large font, top of dashboard, color-coded status. Everything else is supporting context.

2. Real-Time or Near Real-Time

Data that's hours old feels current. Data that's days old feels historical. Data that's weeks old is archaeology. Automate updates.

3. Trend, Don't Just Report

Show: Current value, % change from last period, trend line (up/down/flat), comparison to target. A number alone is meaningless.

Before: "Daily Active Users: 3,247"
After: "DAU: 3,247 (‚Üë8.2% vs last week, Target: 3,500, 93% to goal)"

4. Physically Visible

Best dashboards are ambient‚Äîdisplayed on office screens, pinned in Slack channels, on team homepages. You shouldn't have to remember to check them.

5. Actionable Insights, Not Just Data

Include: What changed? Why? What action should we take? Don't make people interpret raw data.

Dashboard Hierarchy: What to Include

Priority What to Show Why
Tier 1 The OMTM (primary result metric) North Star - everyone should know this number daily
Tier 2 2-3 supporting metrics that drive the OMTM Leading indicators that predict the primary result
Tier 3 Key constraints or guardrails Metrics you can't let decline (quality, cost, etc.)
Not Included Vanity metrics, nice-to-know stats, departmental metrics Dilutes focus, creates noise
SaaS Product Team Dashboard Example:
  • Tier 1: Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) - $127K (‚Üë12% MoM, Target: $150K)
  • Tier 2:
    • New signups this week: 47 (‚Üì5% vs last week)
    • Activation rate: 68% (‚Üë3%, Target: 70%)
    • Churn rate: 3.2% (Flat, Guardrail: <5%)
  • Tier 3: NPS: 52 (Guardrail: >45)

Dashboard Formats and Tools

Physical Displays: TV screens in office showing live dashboards (Geckoboard, Databox, custom builds)

Pros: Ambient visibility, builds culture, impossible to ignore
Cons: Requires office presence, initial setup

Team Channels (Slack/Teams): Automated daily/weekly metric posts

Pros: Reaches remote teams, creates discussion thread, low barrier
Cons: Can get buried in chat noise

Shared Dashboards (Tableau, Looker, Metabase): Bookmarked, live-updating BI tools

Pros: Powerful analytics, drill-down capability, real-time
Cons: Requires active checking, can become overwhelming

Simple Spreadsheets (Google Sheets): Manually updated, highly visible

Pros: No technical setup, everyone can access, forces manual review
Cons: Labor-intensive, prone to staleness

Best practice: Start simple (Slack + spreadsheet), automate as you grow (BI tools), make ambient when possible (physical displays).

Making Dashboards Drive Action

Daily Check-In Ritual: Start standup by reviewing the dashboard. "What's changed? Are we on track? What needs attention today?"

Red/Yellow/Green Alerts: Automate alerts when metrics cross thresholds (red: urgent action needed, yellow: watch closely, green: on track).

Commentary Layer: Add brief explanations when metrics change significantly. "DAU dropped 15% yesterday due to server outage, recovered to normal today."

Ownership Tags: Assign each metric an owner who explains anomalies and proposes responses.

Bain & Company study: Teams with visible, real-time performance dashboards improved goal achievement rates by 30% vs. teams with monthly reporting only. Daily visibility created daily course correction.

Lesson 3: Results Review Rhythms

Cadence Creates Culture: Teams that review results regularly develop results orientation. Teams that review sporadically develop results amnesia. Frequency matters more than depth.

The Review Rhythm Hierarchy

Different cadences serve different purposes. High-performing teams operate at multiple frequencies simultaneously.

Frequency Purpose Duration Focus
Daily Spot issues, maintain awareness 5-10 min What's off track today? Quick fixes.
Weekly Course correct, identify trends 30-45 min Are we on pace? What's working/not working?
Monthly Deep analysis, strategic adjustments 90-120 min Why did we hit/miss? What needs to change?
Quarterly Reset goals, major pivots Half day Did we achieve the result? What's next quarter's OMTM?

Daily Results Check-In (5-10 Minutes)

Format: Start of standup or async Slack post

Agenda:

  1. Review the OMTM: Where are we today vs. yesterday?
  2. Flag any alerts: Red/yellow metrics that need attention
  3. One-sentence explanation: Why did it change?
  4. Quick action: Does anything need immediate response?
Daily Check-In Example:

OMTM Update: MRR at $127.5K (‚Üë$500 yesterday, on pace for $150K goal)
Alert: Signups dropped to 4 yesterday (usual: 8-10) - holiday weekend
Action: Monitor today; if pattern continues, flag for weekly review

Total time: 2 minutes

Key principle: Daily reviews are about awareness, not analysis. Spot the signal, defer deep dives.

Weekly Results Review (30-45 Minutes)

Format: Standing weekly meeting, same day/time

Agenda:

  1. Results snapshot (5 min): Did we hit weekly targets? Where are we vs. monthly goal?
  2. Trend analysis (10 min): What's improving? What's declining? What's driving changes?
  3. Wins & losses (10 min): What worked this week? What failed? What did we learn?
  4. Next week priorities (10 min): What are the 1-3 actions to improve results next week?
  5. Commitments (5 min): Who's doing what by when?
Critical rule: Never skip the weekly review. Consistency builds discipline. Skipping once signals results don't matter.
Weekly Review Template:

Week of Nov 18-24 Results:
‚Ä¢ OMTM: MRR grew $3.5K (target: $4K) - 88% of goal
‚Ä¢ Signups: 47 (target: 50) - MISS
‚Ä¢ Activation: 68% (target: 70%) - MISS
‚Ä¢ Churn: 3.2% (guardrail: <5%) - PASS

What worked: Email campaign drove 15 signups (vs. usual 10)
What didn't: Onboarding still not converting enough users to paid
Next week focus: A/B test new onboarding flow, target 70%+ activation
Owner: @Emily ships test by Tuesday

Monthly Deep Dive (90-120 Minutes)

Format: Scheduled meeting, full team attendance required

Agenda:

  1. Month in review (15 min): Did we hit the goal? By how much? Show the data.
  2. Root cause analysis (30 min): Why did we succeed/fail? Dig into the drivers.
  3. What we learned (20 min): What surprised us? What assumptions were wrong?
  4. Strategic adjustments (20 min): Do we need to change approach, metrics, or goals?
  5. Next month plan (15 min): What's the target? What's the strategy?

Key difference from weekly: Monthly reviews go deeper. Weekly asks "are we on track?" Monthly asks "is our strategy working?"

Monthly Review Question Prompts:
  • What was our biggest win this month? Can we replicate it?
  • What was our biggest failure? What would we do differently?
  • Which assumptions proved wrong? What does that tell us?
  • Are we measuring the right things? Should we change our OMTM?
  • Who excelled this month? How do we recognize them?

Quarterly Business Review (Half Day)

Format: Off-site or dedicated block, no interruptions

Agenda:

  1. Quarter results (30 min): Comprehensive review of all goals, context, and outcomes
  2. Strategic retrospective (60 min): What worked at a strategic level? What needs to change?
  3. Next quarter planning (90 min): New OMTM, supporting goals, resource allocation
  4. Team health check (30 min): How's morale? Burnout? Engagement?

Output: Clear quarterly goals, defined metrics, explicit strategy, aligned team.

Common Review Rhythm Mistakes

1. Inconsistent Cadence: Weekly reviews that happen "when we have time" aren't weekly. Consistency signals importance.

2. Too Much Reporting, Not Enough Discussion: If reviews are just presenting data, they fail. The point is interpretation and action.

3. No Pre-Work: If people see the data for the first time in the meeting, you waste time on comprehension instead of discussion. Share data 24 hours ahead.

4. No Follow-Up: Reviews that end without clear commitments and owners = meetings, not accountability sessions.

5. Punitive Tone: If reviews become blame sessions, people stop being honest about problems. Focus on learning, not finger-pointing.

Best practice: Start every review by celebrating wins (even small ones). This creates psychological safety to discuss misses honestly.

üìö Research: Review Frequency and Performance

Gallup study: Teams with weekly goal reviews were 2.5x more likely to hit targets than teams with monthly-only reviews. Frequent feedback loops enable faster course correction.

McKinsey research: High-performing organizations review KPIs 3-5x more frequently than average performers. Speed of learning correlates with frequency of review.

Key insight: More frequent reviews (daily/weekly) beat less frequent deep dives (monthly/quarterly) for sustained performance.
