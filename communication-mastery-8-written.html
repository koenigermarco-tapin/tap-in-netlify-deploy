üìß Email Best Practices

Your inbox is a battlefield for attention. Every email you send competes with hundreds of others for precious mental space. The difference between a message that gets action and one that gets ignored often comes down to how you structure it.

Start with the subject line‚Äîit's your email's first impression. A good subject line tells the reader exactly what they need to know and what action you need. "Q3 Budget Review - Decision Needed by Friday" beats "Quick question" every time. Be specific, include deadlines, and front-load the important information.

In the body, lead with your ask or key information. Busy people skim emails from top to bottom, and many won't make it past the first paragraph. Use the "Bottom Line Up Front" (BLUF) approach: state what you need in the first sentence, then provide context below. This respects your reader's time and increases your response rate dramatically.

Subject: Hey Hi team, wanted to touch base about something...

‚úÖ Clear Subject Line

Subject: [Action Required] Q3 Budget - Approval by Dec 1 Decision needed: Please approve the attached Q3 budget by December 1st...

üí¨ Slack & Chat Etiquette

Instant messaging has revolutionized workplace communication, but it's also created new challenges. The informality of Slack can lead to misunderstandings, notification fatigue, and the expectation of immediate responses that fragments deep work.

Master the art of threading. When your message relates to an ongoing discussion, reply in the thread‚Äînot in the main channel. This keeps conversations organized and reduces noise for teammates who aren't involved. Use @mentions strategically: @channel should be rare (it notifies everyone), while direct @mentions signal who actually needs to respond.

Set expectations about your availability. Use status messages to communicate when you're in deep work, in meetings, or offline. This prevents the anxiety of unanswered messages and gives your team context about response times. Remember: Slack is asynchronous by design, even if it feels synchronous.

Pro Tip: The "No Hello" Rule

Don't send "Hi" and wait for a response. Instead, send your complete question or request in one message. This saves multiple back-and-forths and respects everyone's time. Your message should be self-contained and actionable.

üìù Documentation That Gets Read

Most documentation fails not because of bad content, but because of bad structure. When someone opens your document, they're usually looking for a specific piece of information‚Äînot reading cover to cover. Design your documents for scanning, not studying.

Use clear headings that tell readers exactly what each section contains. Break up long paragraphs into bullet points where possible. Lead each section with the most important information. Include a TL;DR (Too Long; Didn't Read) summary at the top of longer documents so busy readers can get the key points in 30 seconds.

Think about your future reader. Will they understand the context six months from now? Will a new team member know what this document is about? Include enough background to make the document self-contained, but not so much that it buries the key information.

  • Start with a clear title and one-sentence summary
  • Use headings every 2-3 paragraphs
  • Bold key terms and important information
  • Include a "Last Updated" date
  • Link to related documents instead of duplicating content

üé≠ Tone in Written Communication

Without body language and vocal cues, written messages are easily misinterpreted. A direct message can seem curt. A casual message can seem unprofessional. The same words can land very differently depending on the reader's mood and context.

When in doubt, err on the side of warmth. A simple "Thanks for your patience" or "I appreciate you flagging this" can transform a neutral message into a positive interaction. However, don't over-apologize or hedge so much that your message loses clarity. The goal is warmth without weakness.

Read your messages aloud before sending‚Äîespecially important ones. If it sounds harsh or could be misread, soften it. Consider emoji strategically (appropriate in Slack, less so in formal emails). And remember: criticism and sensitive feedback should almost never happen in writing. Pick up the phone or schedule a video call instead.

"You missed the deadline. The report should have been submitted yesterday. Please send it immediately."

‚úÖ Same Message, Better Tone

"I noticed the report wasn't in yet‚Äîis everything okay? We need it for the leadership meeting tomorrow. Let me know if you need any help getting it across the finish line."
