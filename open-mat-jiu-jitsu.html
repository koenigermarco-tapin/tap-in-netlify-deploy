The Jiu-Jitsu Approach to Leadership

In Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu, the smaller person can defeat a larger opponent not through force, but through leverage, timing, and technique. The same principle applies to leadership.

"The best leaders don't force compliance. They create conditions where the right thing becomes the natural thing." ‚Äî Jocko Willink

1. Use Leverage, Not Force

Instead of pushing harder when you meet resistance, look for the leverage point. Where can a small input create a large output? In teams, this often means finding what people already care about and aligning your goals with theirs.

2. Flow with Resistance

When someone pushes back, don't push harder. Redirect their energy. In jiu-jitsu, if someone pushes you, you pull them. If they pull, you push. In leadership, this means turning objections into opportunities to understand and adapt.

3. Position Before Submission

Jiu-jitsu practitioners know: establish good position first, then think about the finish. In leadership, this means building trust and credibility before asking for commitment. Create the conditions for success before demanding results.

4. Tap Early, Tap Often

In training, tapping out (admitting defeat) lets you learn without injury. Leaders who can say "I don't know" or "I was wrong" create psychologically safe teams where everyone learns faster.

5. Technical Precision Over Strength

A perfectly executed technique requires less energy than brute force. Similarly, clear communication, well-designed processes, and aligned incentives achieve more than micromanagement and constant pressure.

Your Practice

This week, identify one situation where you're using force (pushing, demanding, insisting). Ask yourself:

  • What am I resisting that I should flow with?
  • Where's the leverage point I'm missing?
  • What position do I need to establish first?
