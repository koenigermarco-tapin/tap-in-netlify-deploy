Lesson 1: Building Results-Oriented Culture

Culture = What Happens When You're Not Looking: A results-oriented culture means the team prioritizes outcomes over activity, even when no one is watching. It's self-sustaining, not manager-dependent.

What Results-Oriented Culture Looks Like

Observable Behaviors:

  • Team members can recite the primary result without hesitation
  • Decisions get filtered through "does this drive the result?" lens automatically
  • People challenge work that doesn't serve the outcome, regardless of hierarchy
  • Celebrations focus on outcomes achieved, not tasks completed
  • Failures are analyzed through "what result did we miss and why?" not "who's to blame?"
Culture Test:

Ask a new team member after 2 weeks: "What's the team's top priority?"

Results-oriented culture: "We're driving conversion from 22% to 25% by Dec 31. Everything we do serves that goal."
Activity-oriented culture: "We're shipping features, fixing bugs, improving the product... lots of things."

Clarity test = culture test.

The 5 Pillars of Results-Oriented Culture

1. Radical Clarity on the Result

Everyone knows the primary result, how it's measured, and why it matters. This isn't hidden in strategy docs‚Äîit's repeated constantly, publicly, unavoidably.

2. Visible Progress Tracking

Results are displayed prominently (dashboards, Slack updates, team meetings). You can't ignore the scoreboard because it's everywhere.

3. Outcome-Based Recognition

Praise and rewards go to people who move the result needle, not people who look busy. Activity without impact gets acknowledged but not celebrated.

4. Psychological Safety to Challenge

Anyone can ask "how does this drive the result?" without being labeled difficult. Questioning misaligned work is encouraged, not punished.

5. Leadership Consistency

Leaders model results focus daily. They say no to distractions publicly, reference the result in every decision, and hold the line under pressure.

Culture = The 5 pillars operating simultaneously. If any pillar is missing, culture degrades to activity orientation.

Building Culture: The 90-Day Embedding Plan

Weeks 1-2: Establish Clarity

  • Define the primary result in one sentence
  • Present it to the team with rationale and measurement plan
  • Get explicit buy-in from every team member
  • Post it everywhere (Slack, dashboards, meeting rooms)

Weeks 3-4: Make It Visible

  • Set up live dashboard showing progress
  • Implement daily standup check-in on the result
  • Start weekly results review ritual

Weeks 5-6: Reinforce Through Recognition

  • Celebrate first wins publicly (even small progress)
  • Call out examples of people prioritizing result over activity
  • Share stories of saying no to protect focus

Weeks 7-8: Build Psychological Safety

  • Encourage questioning: "Is this the highest-leverage work?"
  • Model it yourself: publicly defer low-value work
  • Reward people who challenge misaligned priorities

Weeks 9-12: Sustain Through Repetition

  • Keep referencing the result in every decision
  • Run retrospectives: "Did we stay focused on the result?"
  • Adjust course based on what's working
Real Example - 90-Day Culture Shift:

A product team shifted from "ship features fast" to "drive retention to 85%+". Over 90 days:
  • Week 2: Every meeting started with "current retention: 78%, target: 85%"
  • Week 4: Dashboard on office TV showing daily retention trend
  • Week 6: Celebrated engineer who killed a feature because it didn't impact retention
  • Week 8: PM publicly said no to exec's feature request, team backed them
  • Week 12: New team member independently asked "does this drive retention?" in planning
Result: Retention hit 86% by day 90. Culture embedded.

Common Culture-Building Mistakes

1. Declaring Culture Without Behavioral Change

"We're now results-oriented!" ‚Üí but nothing changes in how work is prioritized, recognized, or tracked. Words without systems fail.

2. Inconsistent Leadership

Leader says "focus on results" but celebrates someone who shipped 10 low-impact features. Mixed signals kill culture.

3. Too Many Results

Trying to build culture around 5 priorities = building culture around none. One clear result wins.

4. No Visible Progress

If the team can't see progress daily, urgency fades. Out of sight = out of mind.

5. Punishing Honest Assessment

If people get blamed for saying "we're not on track," they'll stop being honest. Culture requires truth-telling.

Sustaining Culture Long-Term

Rituals that reinforce:

  • Weekly Results Review: Never skip. Consistency signals importance.
  • Onboarding: New hires learn the result on day 1, see it modeled on day 2
  • Performance Reviews: Tie individual performance to contribution to the result
  • Retrospectives: Include "did we stay results-focused?" as a standing question
  • Leadership Storytelling: Share examples of trade-offs made to protect the result
Culture compounds: The first 90 days are hard (constant reinforcement needed). After 6 months, it becomes self-sustaining. After a year, new people get socialized into it automatically.

Lesson 2: Your Results Legacy

The True Test of Leadership: What happens to results after you leave? If everything falls apart without you, you built dependency, not capability. Legacy = systems that outlast you.

What Is a Results Legacy?

Your results legacy is the answer to this question: "If you left tomorrow, would the team still achieve outcomes, or would they revert to activity?"

Two Scenarios:

Scenario A - No Legacy: Leader leaves. Team loses clarity on priorities. Dashboard stops updating. Weekly reviews stop happening. Within 30 days, team is back to shipping features without asking "does this drive the result?"

Scenario B - Strong Legacy: Leader leaves. Team continues daily result check-ins. Dashboard still updated. Someone else runs weekly reviews. New leader walks into a results-oriented culture already operating.

The difference? Scenario B built systems, not just habits.

The 3 Levels of Leadership Impact

Level 1 - Personal Results: You achieve outcomes while you're present and active. The moment you step away, progress stops.

Impact duration: 0 days after you leave

Level 2 - Team Results: Your team achieves outcomes while you're leading them. They've learned the behaviors but haven't internalized the systems.

Impact duration: 30-90 days after you leave (then degrades)

Level 3 - Legacy Results: The systems you built continue generating outcomes long after you're gone. Results orientation is embedded in culture, processes, and team DNA.

Impact duration: Years (potentially indefinitely)

Goal: Reach Level 3. Build systems so robust that your departure is a transition, not a crisis.

The 5 Systems That Create Legacy

1. Results Definition System

How does the team decide what result to pursue? Is there a documented process for setting quarterly goals, choosing the OMTM, and aligning stakeholders?

  • Document the framework (e.g., OKR process, prioritization matrix)
  • Train someone else to run it
  • Run it with them leading while you observe

2. Progress Tracking System

Who maintains the dashboard? Who posts updates? Is it automated or manual? If manual, is the process documented?

  • Automate wherever possible (APIs, integrations)
  • If manual, create step-by-step runbook
  • Rotate ownership quarterly so knowledge spreads

3. Review Rhythm System

Are weekly/monthly reviews on the calendar permanently? Is there a template? Who facilitates if you're not there?

  • Lock recurring meetings in calendar (don't make them optional)
  • Create facilitation guide with agenda and question prompts
  • Rotate facilitator role to build capability

4. Recognition System

How do outcome wins get celebrated? Is there a process for identifying and praising results-oriented behavior?

  • Build recognition into weekly reviews ("wins of the week")
  • Create Slack channel for celebrating result progress
  • Tie performance reviews to outcome contribution (documented criteria)

5. Decision-Making System

How do people decide what to work on? Is the prioritization framework documented and accessible?

  • Write down the framework (e.g., "Does it drive OMTM? Score 1-10. >7 = do, <7 = defer")
  • Make it public (Wiki, Notion, team handbook)
  • Require people to reference it when proposing new work

Building Legacy: The Documentation Habit

Legacy requires writing things down. If it only exists in your head, it dies when you leave.

What to Document Why It Matters Where to Put It

Test: Could a new team member, given only your documentation, understand what result matters and how to contribute? If no, keep documenting.

The Succession Mindset

Great leaders actively develop their replacements. If you're irreplaceable, you've failed to build capability.

Questions to ask yourself:

  • Who could run the weekly results review if I'm out for a month?
  • Who could explain our prioritization framework to a new exec?
  • Who could make the call to say no to a low-priority request?
  • Who could update the dashboard if I left tomorrow?

If the answer is "no one" or "only me": Start teaching. Shadow someone. Let them lead while you observe. Give feedback. Repeat until they own it.

Succession in Action:

A director spent 6 months developing their senior PM to own results culture:
  • Month 1-2: PM shadows director in weekly reviews, takes notes
  • Month 3-4: PM co-facilitates reviews, asks half the questions
  • Month 5: PM facilitates alone, director just observes
  • Month 6: Director doesn't attend, PM runs it fully
When director got promoted, the PM stepped into the role. Results culture didn't skip a beat.

Lesson 3: Teaching Results Orientation

Black Belt Instructor Level: You've mastered results focus. Now you teach others to do the same. Your impact multiplies‚Äîevery leader you develop can build results-oriented teams of their own.

Why Teaching Matters

Individual contributor: Your impact = what you accomplish personally
Leader: Your impact = what your team accomplishes
Teacher/Multiplier: Your impact = what your team accomplishes √ó the leaders they become √ó the teams those leaders build

Teaching results orientation scales your impact exponentially. One great leader you develop might build 5 great teams over their career.

The Multiplication Effect:

You teach 3 leaders results-oriented thinking over 2 years. Each leads a team of 8.
Direct impact: 3 teams = 24 people operating with results focus

Those 3 leaders each develop 2 more leaders over next 3 years. Each of those leads a team of 8.
Multiplied impact: 9 teams = 72 people now operating with results focus

Your teaching created 50+ people focused on outcomes you'll never directly manage.

The 5 Stages of Teaching Results Orientation

Stage 1: Model It (They Watch)

Before teaching explicitly, model results-oriented behavior consistently. Let them see you:

  • Reference the primary result in every decision
  • Say no to distractions publicly
  • Run results reviews weekly
  • Celebrate outcomes, not activity
  • Prioritize ruthlessly

Duration: Ongoing, but especially important in first 30 days with a new leader

Stage 2: Name It (They Learn the Language)

Start labeling what you're doing: "I'm saying no to this because it doesn't drive our primary result." "This dashboard keeps us focused on the outcome that matters." Give them vocabulary.

Duration: Weeks 2-4

Stage 3: Involve Them (They Practice with Support)

Invite them into the process: "Help me run the weekly review." "What do you think we should prioritize?" "How would you handle this trade-off?" Let them practice with you there.

Duration: Weeks 5-8

Stage 4: Coach Them (They Lead, You Guide)

Hand them ownership: "You run the next results review, I'll observe." Afterward, give feedback: "Great job focusing on the outcome. Next time, push back more when someone brings up off-topic work."

Duration: Weeks 9-12

Stage 5: Release Them (They Own It)

Step back. They run results-oriented processes independently. You're available for questions but not involved day-to-day. This is graduation.

Duration: Month 4+

Teaching Frameworks, Not Just Tactics

Don't just teach what to do; teach how to think.

Tactical Teaching Framework Teaching Why Framework Wins
"Track daily active users" "Choose metrics that predict the outcome, not just measure activity" They can apply it to any metric selection decision
"Say no to that feature request" "Use the framework: Does it score 7+ on impact to primary result? If not, defer." They can prioritize independently
"Run weekly reviews" "Frequent review loops enable faster course correction. Pick cadence based on result timeline." They can design review rhythms for any goal
"Celebrate when we hit goals" "Recognize the behavior you want repeated. Outcome wins > activity wins." They know what to reward in their own teams
Principle: Teach the thinking behind the action. Frameworks are portable; tactics are situation-specific.

Common Teaching Mistakes

1. Telling, Not Showing

"You should focus on results!" ‚Üí without modeling it yourself. People learn from behavior, not lectures.

2. No Practice Opportunities

You run everything yourself, they just watch. Learning requires doing.

3. Skipping the "Why"

"Do it this way" without explaining the rationale. They follow blindly but can't adapt when context changes.

4. Not Giving Feedback

They practice but you don't coach. They repeat mistakes because no one corrected them.

5. Holding Too Tight

Never fully releasing ownership. They never develop confidence if you're always hovering.

The Coaching Conversation Structure

When developing leaders in results orientation, use this framework:

1. Ask, Don't Tell (Start with Their Thinking)

"What result are we trying to drive here?" "How would you prioritize these requests?" Let them process first.

2. Reflect Back (Test Understanding)

"So you're saying X would drive Y because Z. Is that right?" Ensure they're thinking clearly.

3. Challenge Assumptions (Deepen Thinking)

"What if that assumption is wrong? How else could we approach this?" Push them to consider alternatives.

4. Offer Framework (Give Mental Model)

"Here's how I think about this: results > activity, leading indicators > lagging, etc." Share your decision-making lens.

5. Let Them Decide (Build Ownership)

"What do you think we should do?" Don't override unless they're making a critical mistake. Let them own the call.

Coaching Example:

Scenario: Junior PM wants to add 5 features to sprint.

You: "What result are we trying to drive this quarter?"
Them: "Increase trial-to-paid conversion to 25%."
You: "Great. How do these 5 features connect to that result?"
Them: "Well... maybe 2 of them help conversion. The others are just nice to have."
You: "What's the prioritization framework we use?"
Them: "Score 1-10 on impact to primary result. Above 7 = do now."
You: "How would you score these 5?"
Them: "The 2 are probably 8s. The other 3 are like 4s or 5s."
You: "What should we do?"
Them: "Focus on the 2 that matter, defer the rest."
You: "Exactly. You just applied the framework. That's how you make the call every time."

Building a Teaching Habit

Weekly Teaching Touchpoints:

  • 1-on-1s: Reserve 10 minutes to coach on one results-oriented decision they're facing
  • Team Meetings: When making a priority call, narrate your reasoning out loud
  • Retrospectives: Ask "What did we learn about staying results-focused this week?"
  • Decision Reviews: After a big call, debrief: "Why did I say no there? What framework did I use?"
Make teaching part of your workflow, not an extra activity. Every decision is a teaching moment if you narrate the thinking.
