Your Reaction Determines What You Receive

Here's a hard truth: the quality of feedback you receive is a direct reflection of how you've responded to it in the past. If you get defensive, justify, or shut down when people give you feedback, they'll stop giving it. You'll be surrounded by silence‚Äîwhich is far more dangerous than criticism.

Leaders who can't handle feedback create cultures where problems fester unsaid. Your team will smile, nod, and slowly disengage. They'll stop taking ownership because you've signaled that your ego is more fragile than the mission.

The test: When was the last time someone on your team gave you critical feedback? If you can't remember, that's the feedback.

The "Thank You" Protocol

No matter how the feedback is delivered‚Äîeven if it's clumsy, poorly timed, or partially wrong‚Äîyour first response should be: "Thank you for telling me."

This doesn't mean you agree. It means you appreciate that they took a risk to tell you something uncomfortable. Most people don't. They'll talk to everyone except you.

What NOT to Say:

  • ‚ùå "But I..." (justification)
  • ‚ùå "You don't understand..." (defensiveness)
  • ‚ùå "Actually..." (correction)
  • ‚ùå "That's because..." (excuses)

What TO Say:

  • ‚úÖ "Thank you for sharing that."
  • ‚úÖ "Tell me more about what you observed."
  • ‚úÖ "What would you recommend?"
  • ‚úÖ "Let me think about that and get back to you."

Asking Clarifying Questions

After receiving feedback, your job is to understand it fully before deciding what to do with it. Ask questions to get specific:

  • "Can you give me an example of when I did that?"
  • "What would you like to see me do differently?"
  • "How often have you noticed this?"
  • "What impact did it have on you/the team?"

These questions show you're taking the feedback seriously without being defensive. They also help you separate vague impressions from actionable insights.

The 24-Hour Processing Rule

When you receive difficult feedback, your nervous system will react before your thinking brain can engage. You might feel defensive, ashamed, angry, or hurt. This is normal.

Give yourself 24 hours to process before deciding what to do. During that time:

  1. Acknowledge the emotion: "I'm feeling defensive right now, and that's okay."
  2. Look for the 2% truth: Even if 98% feels wrong, is there 2% that's accurate?
  3. Consider the source: Does this person see something others see?
  4. Separate useful from not: What can you learn, even if the delivery was poor?

‚ùå Reactive Response

Immediately defend yourself, explain why they're wrong, or dismiss the feedback as unfair.

‚úÖ Thoughtful Response

Say "Thank you, let me think about that." Process for 24 hours. Then decide what action to take.

Growth Mindset vs. Fixed Mindset

Carol Dweck's research shows two mindsets when receiving feedback:

Fixed Mindset: "This feedback means I'm not good enough." You take it as an indictment of your character. You avoid situations where you might receive more feedback.

Growth Mindset: "This feedback shows me where I can improve." You see it as data, not judgment. You actively seek more feedback to accelerate learning.

The difference? Fixed mindset people plateau. Growth mindset people compound their skills year after year.

üéØ Practice Exercise: Reframe Feedback

Think of a piece of feedback you've received recently (or in the past) that you initially resisted. Now reframe it:

  1. What was the feedback?
  2. What was your initial defensive reaction?
  3. Looking back, what's the 2% truth in it?
  4. What could you learn or change?

üí° Key Takeaway

Your response to feedback is more important than the feedback itself. Receive it gracefully, process it thoughtfully, and act on what's useful. This separates leaders who plateau from those who keep growing.
