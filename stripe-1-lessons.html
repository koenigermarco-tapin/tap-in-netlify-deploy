3. Trust Under Pressure

At Just Eat Takeaway, I managed operations for 2,000+ delivery partners across multiple countries. When COVID hit and demand spiked 350%, I learned this lesson fast: the teams that survived weren't the ones with the best plans‚Äîthey were the ones where people felt safe admitting when something wasn't working.

Teams without vulnerability-based trust operate in constant self-protection mode. People hedge their language, hide their mistakes, and avoid asking for help. Energy that should go toward results gets wasted on political maneuvering and impression management.

The breakthrough came when I stopped pretending I had all the answers. In a team call during peak crisis, I said: 'I don't know how to fix this yet. But here's what I'm seeing, here's what I'm thinking, and I need your help.' That moment of vulnerability under pressure created more trust than six months of team-building ever could.

2. Vulnerability + Composure = Trust Under Pressure

Harvard Business School research confirms that trust built through shared vulnerability under stress is 3x stronger than trust built through team dinners or personality tests. Why? Because your brain remembers emotional experiences far better than intellectual ones.

Here's the paradox: We think trust is built by being reliable and having answers. But research shows trust is actually built through appropriate vulnerability‚Äîadmitting what you don't know while maintaining clarity about what you do know. Under pressure, teams need both: 'I don't have the answer AND I'm committed to figuring it out with you.'

The framework for high-pressure trust: (1) Name the reality: 'This is hard and we don't have all the answers yet.' (2) Share what you know: 'Here's what we do know...' (3) Ask for input: 'What am I missing? What do you see?' (4) Commit to action: 'Here's what we'll do in the next hour.'

2. How to Practice Vulnerability

The key is appropriate vulnerability‚Äîsharing uncertainty about the path while maintaining conviction about the destination. Think of it like a pilot during turbulence: 'Folks, we're hitting rough air and I'm adjusting our altitude' is honest and competent. 'I have no idea what's happening!' is panic that destroys confidence.

Start small: In your next team meeting, admit something you don't know or a recent mistake you made. Observe how people respond. Do they reciprocate with their own vulnerability, or does the room go silent? That response tells you about your team's current trust level and where you need to work.

1. The Leader Goes First

Psychological safety doesn't happen by accident. It's created intentionally by leaders who model vulnerability first. Amy Edmondson's research at Harvard is clear: The leader sets the tone. If you admit uncertainty, others will. If you hide mistakes, others will. If you ask for help, others will. If you project false confidence, others will too‚Äîand trust erodes.

The practice: In your next team meeting, try this: 'I made a decision last week that I'm rethinking. Here's what I got wrong, here's what I'm learning, and here's what I'm going to do differently.' That 30-second admission does more for psychological safety than 30 hours of culture presentations.
