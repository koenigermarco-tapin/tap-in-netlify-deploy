Managing Upwards

Managing upwards isn't politics‚Äîit's making your leader's job easier while advancing your goals. It's about alignment.

üéØ Their Pressures

2Ô∏è‚É£ Recommendation

üö´ Surprises

Managing Downwards

Setting people up for success

Start

Managing Downwards

Managing downwards isn't control‚Äîit's clarity and trust. Give people what they need to succeed, then step back.

Set Expectations Clearly
üéØ What Done Looks Like
"Improve customer response time" is vague. "Response within 4 hours, 95% of tickets" is clear. Be specific.
‚è∞ When It's Needed
Deadlines matter. "This week" is ambiguous. "Friday EOD" is not.
üìè Quality Bar
What does good mean to you? For a proposal: "3 pages, executive summary, 3 alternatives." Now they know.
üîó Why It Matters
Context motivates. "This report drives the board discussion on Q4 strategy" matters more than "I need this."
Manage Through Trust, Not Control
ü§ù Assume Competence
Micromanaging signals you don't trust them. Delegating signals you do. People rise to trust.
üéì Coach, Don't Direct
Instead of "Do X," try "What approach are you thinking?" Then guide if they're off track.
‚úÖ Check-ins, Not Surveillance
Regular 1-on-1s catch issues early. Constant status updates feel like surveillance.
Performance Conversations
üí¨ Feedback Early
Don't wait for annual reviews. Weekly feedback prevents surprises.
üéØ Specific Observations
"You dominated the meeting" is vague. "You interrupted 5 times and didn't ask questions" is actionable.
üöÄ Growth Path
Connect feedback to development. "This matters for the lead role you're targeting."
üí° This Week: Clarity Conversation
  • Take one project/task your team is working on
  • Write down: what done looks like, deadline, quality bar, why it matters
  • Share this in your next 1-on-1 or team check-in
  • Notice how clarity reduces back-and-forth

Lateral Relationships

Lateral work is harder than hierarchy‚Äîyou have no authority. Your currency is credibility, reliability, and mutual benefit.

Build Credibility First
‚≠ê Do What You Say
Small commitments kept build trust more than big promises. Consistent reliability beats charisma.
üéØ Be Easy to Work With
Respond to messages. Show up on time. Follow through. No drama. These aren't glamorous, but they're everything.
ü§≤ Give Value First
Help without expecting return. Introduce people. Share knowledge. When you give, people want to reciprocate.
Frame for Their Win
üéØ Their Goal, Not Yours
Lead with what they care about. "This marketing data will help your OKR on customer insights" matters more than your need.
üí™ Make Them Look Good
When you collaborate, share credit generously. "Sarah's marketing input was crucial here." They'll want to work with you again.
ü§ù Understand Constraints
They have priorities, deadlines, limits. Ask "What's your bandwidth?" before asking for help.

1Ô∏è‚É£ Assume Good Intent
They're not against you‚Äîthey just have different priorities. Understand theirs before judging.
2Ô∏è‚É£ Find the Real Gap
"They won't help" usually means "They don't understand why this matters to them." Ask clarifying questions.
3Ô∏è‚É£ Escalate Carefully
Loop in your leader only if direct conversation fails. But always try direct first.
üí° This Week: Lateral Outreach
  • Identify one peer collaboration you need
  • Research their current priorities/OKRs
  • Frame your request in terms of their goals
  • Coffee meeting to build the relationship first
  • Offer help before asking for it
