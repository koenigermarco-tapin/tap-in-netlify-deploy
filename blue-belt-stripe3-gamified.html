üéØ The Feedback Problem

Most feedback either hurts or is useless. There's a better way.

Why Feedback Fails

Most people swing between two extremes: they're either brutally honest (and damage relationships) or they're so nice that their feedback is meaningless (and damage performance).

Neither works. People need feedback that's both kind AND clear. That's harder than it sounds.

ü•ã The BJJ Principle: Honest Sparring

In Jiu-Jitsu, if your partner goes too easy on you, you don't improve. If they go too hard, you get injured. The best training partners push you to your edge without breaking you.

That's what good feedback looks like: honest enough to create growth, delivered in a way that keeps the relationship intact.

"Your job is to care personally while challenging directly." ‚Äî Kim Scott

üìä The Research

Kim Scott, former Google and Apple executive, developed the Radical Candor framework after seeing how feedback failures destroyed teams and careers.

Her research found that 85% of workers have received feedback that was so vague or unclear they couldn't act on it. And 37% have experienced feedback so harsh it damaged their relationship with the giver permanently.

üìä The Radical Candor Framework

Two axes. Four quadrants. One sweet spot.

The Two Dimensions

Care Personally: Do you genuinely care about this person as a human being?

Challenge Directly: Are you willing to tell them the uncomfortable truth?

‚Üê Challenge Directly Challenge Directly ‚Üí

üòä Ruinous Empathy

Care personally, but don't challenge. "You're doing great!" (when they're not). Feels kind, but hurts growth.

üéØ Radical Candor

Care personally AND challenge directly. "I know you can do better. Here's specifically what I'm seeing..."

üòê Manipulative Insincerity

Don't care, don't challenge. False praise or silence. Just getting through the day.

üò§ Obnoxious Aggression

Challenge directly, but don't care personally. "This is terrible. Fix it." Honest, but damaging.

üì§ Giving Feedback Well

Specific, timely, and kind-but-clear.

1. Be Specific, Not General

‚ùå "Your presentation was bad."
‚úÖ "When you skipped the demo, the client looked confused and asked follow-up questions you'd already answered."

2. Focus on Behavior, Not Character

‚ùå "You're careless."
‚úÖ "The last three reports had data errors that required revision."

3. Be Timely

Give feedback as close to the event as possible. Waiting for the annual review means they've practiced the wrong behavior for months.

4. State Your Intent

Start by making your intention clear: "I'm sharing this because I want to help you succeed" or "I care about your growth, which is why I'm saying this."

üì• Receiving Feedback Well

This is harder than giving it.

Your Default Reaction Will Fight You

When you hear critical feedback, your brain goes into defense mode. You want to explain, justify, minimize, or counterattack. This is natural. And it's also the enemy.

The ability to receive feedback without defensiveness is a superpower. Most people can't do it.

1. Listen First

Don't respond immediately. Let them finish. Take a breath. Your first instinct is usually to defend‚Äîdon't.

2. Seek to Understand

Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me an example?" or "What would you have liked to see instead?" You're trying to understand, not to rebut.

3. Thank Them

Even if the feedback stings, thank them for giving it. This encourages more feedback in the future‚Äîwhich is what you want.

4. Reflect Before Reacting

You don't have to agree immediately. Say "Let me think about this" and come back later with a considered response.

ü•ã The Tap as Feedback

In BJJ, the tap is the ultimate feedback: "That worked. I was caught." The best training partners don't get defensive when they tap. They ask: "What did I miss? Where did I go wrong?"

Every tap is a lesson. Every piece of feedback is the same.

üí°
The Real Test
How you respond to feedback determines whether you get more of it. If you're defensive, people will stop being honest. If you're grateful, they'll keep helping you improve.
