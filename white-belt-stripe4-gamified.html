Lesson 1
Coaching Others in Trust-Building
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • How to help others develop their own trust-building skills
  • Coaching questions that surface hidden trust issues
  • When to coach vs when to intervene directly

üìñ Core Concept

You've built trust. Now it's time to teach others to do the same.

Coaching trust-building is different from doing it yourself. Instead of modeling vulnerability, you're helping someone else discover their own capacity for it.

üéØ Key Coaching Questions for Trust Issues

When someone says "I don't trust my team":

  • "What would need to happen for you to trust them 10% more?"
  • "Is there anyone on the team you do trust? What's different about that relationship?"
  • "Have you shared this concern with the team? If not, why not?"

When someone avoids vulnerability:

  • "What's the risk you're protecting against by not sharing that?"
  • "What's one small thing you could share that feels 10% vulnerable?"
  • "What happened in the past when you were vulnerable? How did that shape today?"

When someone struggles to repair broken trust:

  • "What would a good repair look like to you?"
  • "If you were coaching someone else in this situation, what would you tell them?"
  • "What's the smallest first step you could take toward repair?"

üß† Coach vs Intervene

Situation Response
Team member avoids vulnerability in low-stakes moments Coach: Ask questions to help them reflect
Trust breakdown between two colleagues Intervene: Facilitate a repair conversation
Someone struggles to give feedback Coach: Role-play scenarios together
Toxic behavior (using vulnerability as weapon) Intervene: Direct conversation + consequences if needed

üìä Coaching Effectiveness Research

Google's Manager Research shows that leaders who coach (rather than direct) have teams with 17% higher psychological safety scores. The key distinction: Coaches ask questions that help people discover their own answers. Directors tell people what to do.

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Coaching trust means asking questions that help others discover their own capacity
  • Effective coaching questions surface hidden fears and assumptions
  • Coach in low-stakes situations; intervene when safety or culture is at risk

üìñ Core Concept

Trust exercises are great‚Äîbut if they're the only time the team practices vulnerability, they won't stick.

Real trust culture means embedding vulnerability into daily operations: hiring, onboarding, meetings, feedback, promotions.

  • Make feedback bidirectional‚Äîleaders receive feedback from team members publicly

Promotions:

  • Include "psychological safety created" as a promotion criterion

Lesson 3
Trust Under Pressure
Start

üéØ What You'll Learn

  • Why trust often collapses during high-stakes moments
  • How to maintain psychological safety when stakes are high
  • The "pressure test" for team trust

üìñ Core Concept

Trust is easy when nothing's at stake. The real test comes under pressure: tight deadlines, critical decisions, public failures.

High-performing teams don't lose trust when stakes rise‚Äîthey rely on it even more.

ü•ã The BJJ Analogy: Rolling Under Pressure

In BJJ, there's a difference between drilling techniques (low pressure) and live rolling (high pressure). Some people are great training partners during drills, but become reckless or overly aggressive when rolling.

The same happens in teams. Low-pressure vulnerability is easy. High-pressure vulnerability (admitting you don't have the answer when everyone's watching) is where trust is truly tested.

‚ö†Ô∏è How Trust Breaks Under Pressure

Pressure Scenario Trust-Breaking Response Trust-Building Response
Missed deadline on critical project Blame others, hide the delay, panic "We're behind. Here's why. What can we adjust?"
Executive asks for update in public meeting Fabricate progress, throw team under bus "We hit a blocker. Team is working on it. I'll update you Friday."
Team member makes costly error Public reprimand, loss of confidence "Let's debrief privately. What happened? How do we prevent this?"
Conflicting priorities from leadership Say yes to everything, burn team out "We can do A or B well, not both. Which matters more?"

üìä Amy Edmondson's Teaming Research

Edmondson's study of hospital surgical teams found that high-performing teams under pressure (emergency surgeries) had leaders who explicitly verbalized uncertainty: "I'm not sure what we're dealing with yet. Let me know what you're seeing." This maintained psychological safety even in life-or-death situations.

üõ†Ô∏è The Pressure Test

Want to know if your team has real trust? Run this diagnostic:

  1. Simulate pressure: Create a high-stakes scenario (e.g., rapid decision-making exercise with incomplete information)
  2. Observe behaviors: Do people admit confusion? Ask for help? Or do they pretend confidence and hide struggles?
  3. Debrief: "What happened to our vulnerability when stakes went up? Why?"

If trust collapses under simulated pressure, it will absolutely collapse under real pressure.

üí™ Building Pressure Resilience

  • Practice high-stakes vulnerability: Run pressure tests regularly so it becomes muscle memory
  • Leader modeling: Leaders must visibly maintain vulnerability under pressure ("I don't have all the answers here")
  • Normalize struggle: Create language for pressure: "This is a high-pressure moment. Let's stay connected."

üí° Key Takeaways

  • Trust is tested most when stakes are high‚Äîpressure reveals true team culture
  • High-performing teams maintain vulnerability even when consequences are real
  • Simulated pressure tests reveal whether your team's trust is real or performative

‚ú® Practice Exercise

This week: The next time your team faces a high-pressure moment (deadline, difficult decision, unexpected problem), pause and verbalize your own uncertainty.

Say: "I'm not sure of the best path forward. What are others seeing?" Notice if anyone reciprocates with their own uncertainty.

üéØ What You'll Learn

üìñ Assessment Overview

The White Belt Assessment isn't a test you can cram for. It's a demonstration of embodied trust-building capability.

You'll encounter scenarios, reflective prompts, and a practical demonstration that reveals whether you've truly integrated the lessons‚Äîor just memorized them.

üìã What to Expect

  • Part 1: Scenario-Based Questions (15 scenarios) - Realistic trust challenges. You'll choose responses and explain your reasoning.
  • Part 2: Reflective Prompts (3 prompts) - Write about your own trust journey. What have you learned? How have you changed?
  • Part 3: Practical Demonstration (1 exercise) - Design a trust intervention for a real or hypothetical team challenge.

Passing Score: 80% (12/15 scenarios correct + thoughtful reflections + coherent intervention design)

üß† How to Prepare

  1. Review your practice exercises: Go back through the 16 lessons. What did you actually do? What worked? What didn't?
  2. Reflect on real experiences: Think about moments when you practiced vulnerability, ran the Grip-Switch drill, or repaired broken trust. Those are your evidence.
  3. Don't memorize‚Äîembody: The assessment isn't about reciting Lencioni's framework. It's about knowing what to do when trust is breaking down in real time.

üìä Mastery vs. Memorization

Cognitive science distinguishes between declarative knowledge ("I know that vulnerability builds trust") and procedural knowledge ("I know how to be vulnerable in this specific moment").

The White Belt Assessment tests procedural knowledge. You'll pass if you can actually do the thing, not just explain it.

ü•ã What White Belt Means

In BJJ, a white belt isn't an expert. It's someone who's learned the fundamentals and can now apply them under pressure.

Earning your White Belt in Tap-In means:

  • You understand why trust matters (Lencioni's framework)
  • You've practiced vulnerability in real situations
  • You can coach others and build trust systems
  • You're ready to move to Blue Belt: Mastering Conflict

üéØ After the White Belt

Once you earn your White Belt, you'll unlock Blue Belt: Mastering Fear of Conflict. This builds on trust‚Äîyou can't have productive conflict without psychological safety first.

The belt journey:

üí° Key Takeaways

  • The White Belt Assessment tests embodied capability, not memorized concepts
  • Preparation means reflecting on real practice, not cramming theory
  • White Belt mastery unlocks Blue Belt: the next stage of team dysfunction mastery
