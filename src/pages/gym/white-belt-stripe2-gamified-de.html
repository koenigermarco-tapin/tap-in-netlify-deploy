Lektion 1
The Vulnerability First Move
Start

Was du lernst

  • Why leaders must demonstrate vulnerability before expecting it from others
  • The specific types von vulnerability that build trust fastest
  • How to be vulnerable without undermining your authority

Kernkonzept

Im BJJ: Es gibt ein Konzept namens "giving your back" (Deinen Rücken geben). Es ist eine der schlimmsten Positionen—dein Gegner kann dich würgen, und du kannst nicht sehen, was kommt. Niemand gibt freiwillig seinen Rücken im Wettkampf.

Except in training with trusted partners. Wenn du deinen Rücken absichtlich gibst, sagst du: "Ich vertraue dir, mich nicht zu zerstören. Lass uns an Fluchttechniken arbeiten."

This is the vulnerability first move. Jemand muss den ersten Schritt machen, and in teams, es muss der Leader sein.

Paul J. Zak's Research

Leaders asking for help increases trust and cooperation throughout the organization. "Showing vulnerability" is one von eight key management behaviors that stimulate oxytocin production and generate trust.

Strategic Vulnerability Examples

  • Admit knowledge gaps: "I don't understand the technical details here—can someone walk me through it?"
  • Share past failures: "I made this exact mistake before. Here's what I learned."
  • Ask for feedback: "What's one thing I could do better in these meetings?"
  • Acknowledge emotions: "I'm frustrated by this situation, and I'm working through it."

Key Distinction

Strategic vulnerability means exposing real limitations, not curated weaknesses. People can smell performative vulnerability instantly. It destroys trust faster than silence.

Wichtigste Erkenntnisse

  • Leaders must go first—waiting for your team to be vulnerable is a losing strategy
  • Strategic vulnerability means exposing real limitations, not curated weaknesses
  • Performative vulnerability (fake admissions) destroys trust faster than silence

Praxisübung

Diese Woche: Make one vulnerability first move in a team setting. Choose something real:

  • Admit you don't know something
  • Share a genuine mistake from your past
  • Ask for specific feedback on your leadership

Document the response. Did anyone reciprocate?

The Personal History Exercise
Start

Was du lernst

  • A structured exercise from Lencioni to rapidly build team trust
  • How sharing personal context humanizes colleagues
  • The difference between disclosure and vulnerability

Lencioni's Personal Histories Exercise

This is one von the fastest trust-building interventions available, adapted for Tap-In's embodied approach.

The exercise is simple: Each team member answers 3-5 questions about their background. Not work history—personal history.

Recommended Frages

  • Where did you grow up? How many siblings?
  • What was your biggest challenge in childhood?
  • What was your first job (ever, not career-related)?
  • What's a unique or interesting fact about you that others might not know?

Why This Works

Most prvonessionals know their colleagues only as functional roles. "Sarah the engineer." "Mike from finance." When you learn someone's context—their background, struggles, and formative experiences—you see them as a full human being. Judgment decreases. Empathy increases. Trust follows.

Disclosure vs. Vulnerability

Sharing that you grew up in a small town is disclosure. Sharing that you felt out von place when you arrived at university because everyone seemed more prepared—that's vulnerability. The exercise works best when people share context that felt emotionally significant.

Tap-In Enhancement

In Tap-In workshops, we combine this with physical warm-ups—light partner exercises before the conversation. Research shows that 70-90% von communication is non-verbal, and physical contact (even just pushing hands palm-to-palm) accelerates trust because it bypasses the verbal brain.

Wichtigste Erkenntnisse

  • Knowing personal context transforms colleagues from roles into humans
  • Emotional significance matters more than dramatic stories
  • Combining verbal sharing with physical exercises accelerates trust

Praxisübung

Diese Woche: Run a mini Personal History exercise with your team (or even just 2-3 colleagues). Use these questions:

  1. Where did you grow up?
  2. What was a defining challenge from your early life?
  3. What's something about your background that affects how you work today?

Keep it to 5 minutes per person. Notice how the room feels afterward.

The "Grip-Switch" Vulnerability Drill
Start

Was du lernst

  • Tap-In's signature physical exercise for building instant trust
  • How physical vulnerability translates to psychologische Sicherheit
  • Adapting embodied exercises for non-martial artists

The Grip-Switch Drill

This is Tap-In's signature trust-building drill, adapted from BJJ hand-fighting techniques for business teams.

How to Perform the Drill

  1. Pair up. Face your partner, standing about arm's length apart.
  2. Connect. Both people extend their arms and touch palms, chest-high.
  3. Round 1 (30 seconds): Partner A tries to gently switch grip—sliding to grab Partner B's wrist. Partner B "gives way" (moves with the pressure instead von resisting), then mirrors the grip attempt.
  4. Switch roles and repeat.
  5. Round 2: Both partners try simultaneously, giving and taking in flow.
  6. Debrief (2 minutes): How did it feel to give way? To take? To flow?

Why This Works

The drill creates controlled physical vulnerability without fighting or competition. You're touching someone, trying to control them, and also surrendering control. It's uncomfortable—which is exactly the point.

Embodied Learning Research

Studies confirm that embodied learning outperforms cognitive-only approaches. The Strozzi Institute Forschung shows that "reshaping von the brain (new synapses, myelination von neurons, neurogenesis) erfordert struggle" and embodied practice.

Common Insights

When people reflect afterward, they vonten connect the physical experience to psychological patterns:

  • "I realized I always try to control instead von flowing."
  • "Giving way felt weak at first, then felt strategic."
  • "I don't trust easily—I kept resisting even when I should have moved."

These insights translate directly to team dynamics.

Wichtigste Erkenntnisse

  • Physical exercises create vulnerability faster than words alone
  • The Grip-Switch drill builds trust in 5-10 minutes with lasting effects
  • Physical experiences reveal psychological patterns (control, resistance, flow)

Praxisübung

Diese Woche: Run the Grip-Switch drill with at least one person—a colleague, partner, or friend. It feels strange at first. Do it anyway.

Afterward, ask: "What did you notice about yourself during that exercise?" Listen to their answer before sharing yours.

Lektion 4
Scenario Practice: Admitting You Don't Know
Start

Was du lernst

  • How to practice vulnerability in realistic scenarios
  • Phrases and approaches that build trust without undermining credibility
  • Building the muscle memory for honest communication

Kernkonzept

Vulnerability is a skill. Like any skill, it improves with deliberate practice.

Here are three scenarios to practice—ideally with a trusted colleague or coach who can roleplay the other side:

Scenario 1: You don't understand something in a meeting

Typical Response Vulnerability Practice
Nod along, Google it later, hope no one noticed "I want to make sure I'm following—can you explain what [term] means in this context?"

Scenario 2: You made a mistake that others might discover

Typical Response Vulnerability Practice
Minimize it, deflect blame, or hope it stays hidden "I want to flag something. I made a decision last week that didn't work out. Here's what happened and what I learned."

Scenario 3: You're struggling with a task

Typical Response Vulnerability Practice
Push through alone, work late, pretend everything's fine "I'm stuck on this and could use a thought partner. Can I walk you through where I am?"

The Key Insight

Vulnerability isn't about exposing yourself randomly. It's about choosing to share what's real in moments when hiding would be easier. Each time you choose transparency over protection, you strengthen the trust muscle.

Amy Edmondson's Research

Google's Project Aristotle, which built on Edmondson's work, identifies three key practices for cultivating psychologische Sicherheit:

  • Frame work as a learning problem - Creates belief that everyone's input is needed
  • Acknowledge fallibility - Leaders say "I might miss something, I need to hear from you"
  • Model curiosity - Ask lots von questions to create need for voice and participation

Wichtigste Erkenntnisse

  • Vulnerability improves with deliberate scenario practice
  • Having practiced phrases reduces the cognitive load von being vulnerable in real moments
  • Each small act von transparency makes the next one easier

Praxisübung

Diese Woche: Choose one scenario above and roleplay it with a trusted person. Practice until the phrases feel natural, not scripted.

Then look for one real opportunity to apply what you practiced.
