Lesson 1: Results vs. Activity Under Pressure

The Pressure Paradox: When stress increases, teams often abandon results focus and retreat to activity metrics. Feeling busy becomes a substitute for being effective.

Why Pressure Kills Results Focus

Psychological Safety of Activity: When outcomes are uncertain, activity feels controllable. "I can't guarantee we'll hit revenue, but I can guarantee I'll make 50 sales calls."

Immediate Feedback Loop: Activity gives instant gratification (task completed!). Results take time to materialize. Under pressure, teams crave the dopamine of crossing things off lists.

Visibility Bias: Looking busy is easier to demonstrate than driving outcomes. When leadership is watching, teams optimize for visible effort.

Analysis Paralysis Avoidance: Focusing on results requires thinking, prioritization, and trade-offs. Activity requires just execution. When overwhelmed, execution feels easier.

Pressure-Driven Activity Trap:

Scenario: Product team is behind on quarterly revenue goal with 3 weeks left.

Activity Response: "Let's ship 10 features this sprint!" (feels productive, easy to count)
Results Response: "Which 2 features will most directly drive conversions? Let's perfect those." (harder to decide, but outcome-focused)

Activity response ships more features. Results response ships more revenue.

The Activity Addiction Cycle

Stage 1 - Pressure Increases: Deadline looms, goal at risk, stakeholders anxious

Stage 2 - Team Panics: "We need to do MORE" becomes the rallying cry

Stage 3 - Activity Explosion: Everyone works longer hours, attends more meetings, ships more tasks

Stage 4 - False Progress: Metrics show activity up (tickets closed, hours logged, meetings held)

Stage 5 - Results Unchanged: All that effort, but the outcome metric barely moves

Stage 6 - Burnout & Blame: Team exhausted, frustrated, looking for external explanations

The trap: Activity feels like progress. It's measurable, visible, and controllable. But activity without strategic focus is just expensive motion.

Maintaining Results Focus Under Pressure: The Framework

1. Restate the Result (Loudly and Often)

When pressure hits, teams forget the goal. Constant repetition: "Our ONLY objective is to increase trial-to-paid conversion to 25% by Dec 15th. Everything else is secondary."

2. Kill the Distraction List

Make a "Not Doing" list explicitly. What tasks, meetings, initiatives are we pausing until the result is achieved?

Not Doing List Example:
  • No new feature requests until conversion goal hit
  • No exploratory projects or R&D
  • Pause weekly all-hands (async update instead)
  • Defer hiring activities to next month
This creates capacity and focus for the result that matters.

3. Daily Results Check-In (30 Seconds)

Every morning: "Conversion rate yesterday: 22%. Gap to goal: 3%. What's the ONE action today that closes that gap?"

4. Pre-Commit Decision Criteria

Before accepting ANY new work: "Does this directly contribute to our primary result? If no, default answer is 'not now.'"

5. Celebrate Progress on the Result, Not Activity

Don't praise "we shipped 5 features!" Praise "conversion moved from 22% to 23% this week!" Reward what you want repeated.

The "Why Are We Doing This?" Test

When pressure is high, implement a forcing function: Before any task/meeting/initiative, someone must answer:

  1. "What result does this drive?" (specific outcome, not "it's good to do")
  2. "How do we measure if it worked?" (observable change in metrics)
  3. "Is this the highest leverage use of time right now?" (compared to alternatives)
Application Example:

Request: "Let's do a team offsite next week to boost morale."
Test Response:
1. What result? ‚Üí "Better team cohesion" (vague, not tied to primary goal)
2. How measure? ‚Üí "Can't really measure it..." (red flag)
3. Highest leverage? ‚Üí "No, we need to focus on conversion experiments"
Decision: Defer offsite until after goal achieved.

When Activity IS the Right Response

Activity isn't always wrong. Sometimes high volume is the strategy:

  • Exploration phase: Don't know what works yet ‚Üí run many experiments quickly
  • Sales ramp: Need pipeline volume ‚Üí more calls IS the result driver
  • Crisis response: System down ‚Üí high-activity recovery mode appropriate

The difference: Strategic activity (chosen because it drives the result) vs. panic activity (done because it feels productive).

üìö Research: Pressure and Performance

Stanford study on "stress and goal orientation": Under high pressure, 73% of teams shifted focus from outcomes to tasks. Teams that maintained outcome focus through explicit reminders and daily check-ins sustained performance 40% better.

Harvard research: "Busyness" correlates negatively with results achievement. Most productive teams worked fewer hours but with tighter focus on high-leverage activities.

Key insight: Pressure doesn't require more work; it requires clearer focus.

New BJJ students, when in danger, "spazz out"‚Äîthey thrash, use all their energy, try everything at once. It feels productive but accomplishes nothing (and burns them out).

Experienced grapplers under pressure stay focused on the escape path that will work. They don't waste energy on random movement. Calm, strategic, focused on the outcome (escape mount), not the activity (moving a lot).

Leadership under pressure is the same: Strategic focus beats frantic activity every time.

üí™ Practice: Pressure Audit

Think about a recent high-pressure period on your team. Honestly assess:

  1. Did you measure success by hours worked or outcomes achieved?
  2. Did the team add more tasks to the backlog or ruthlessly cut scope?
  3. Could you articulate the primary result you were chasing in one sentence?
  4. What % of effort went to activities that didn't directly drive that result?
  5. Did you celebrate being busy or making progress on the goal?

Action: Next time pressure hits, implement the "Restate the Result Daily" practice. Every morning standup: What's the result? Where are we? What's the gap?
