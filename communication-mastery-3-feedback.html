Why Most Feedback Fails

"You need to be more professional." "That presentation wasn't great." "You're not a team player." These are real examples of feedback people have received‚Äîand they're all terrible. They're vague, judgmental, and leave the recipient confused about what to actually change.

Bad feedback damages relationships, creates defensiveness, and wastes everyone's time. Good feedback is specific, observable, and actionable. It focuses on behavior, not character. It describes impact, not intent.

The paradox: Leaders say "my team can't take feedback," but what they really mean is "I don't know how to give it effectively." The problem isn't the receiver‚Äîit's the delivery.

The SBI Model: Situation-Behavior-Impact

Developed by the Center for Creative Leadership, SBI is a simple framework that makes feedback concrete and non-threatening:

S - Situation

Set the context. When and where did this happen? This grounds the conversation in a specific moment, not a character judgment.

Example: "In yesterday's client meeting at 2pm..."

B - Behavior

Describe what you observed, not what you interpreted. Stick to facts that a camera would have captured. Avoid "you always" or "you never."

Example: "...you interrupted Sarah three times while she was presenting the timeline."

I - Impact

Explain the effect of the behavior. How did it affect the outcome, the team, or the relationship? This helps the person understand why it matters.

Example: "This made Sarah lose her train of thought, and the client didn't get the full context they needed to make a decision."

‚ùå Without SBI

"You're always interrupting people. You need to be more respectful and let others finish their thoughts."

‚úÖ With SBI

"In yesterday's meeting (S), you interrupted Sarah three times (B), which made her lose her train of thought and confused the client (I). How can we make sure everyone gets heard?"

Timing and Context Matter

The best feedback is timely, private, and expected:

  • Timely: Within 24-48 hours while it's fresh, but not in the heat of emotion
  • Private: Never criticize in public‚Äîpraise publicly, coach privately
  • Expected: Create a culture where feedback is normal, not a surprise attack

Ask permission before giving feedback: "Can I share some observations from the meeting?" This gives them a moment to prepare mentally and signals respect.

The 3:1 Rule

Research by John Gottman shows that high-performing relationships (and teams) have a ratio of at least 3 positive interactions for every 1 corrective one. This doesn't mean sugarcoating‚Äîit means building a foundation of trust so corrective feedback lands.

If the only time you give feedback is when something's wrong, people will start avoiding you. They'll hide problems, stop taking risks, and disengage.

Pro tip: Catch people doing things right. "That's exactly what I needed" is feedback too‚Äîand it's often more powerful than criticism.

Common Feedback Mistakes to Avoid

  • The Sandwich: Hiding criticism between compliments feels manipulative. People learn to distrust your praise.
  • The Judgment: "You don't care about quality" vs. "I noticed three errors in the report." Describe behavior, not character.
  • The Vague: "Be more proactive" vs. "I'd like you to send me a weekly update by Friday." Be specific.
  • The Laundry List: Bringing up six months of issues at once. Focus on one behavior at a time.
  • The Comparison: "Sarah would have handled this better." Never compare people‚Äîit breeds resentment.
  • The Mind Read: "You obviously don't care." You can't know intent. Stick to observable behavior.

  1. Situation: When and where did this happen?
  2. Behavior: What did you observe (facts only)?
  3. Impact: What was the effect?

Good feedback is a gift. It helps people grow, improves performance, and builds trust. The SBI framework makes it specific, actionable, and kind‚Äîthe opposite of most feedback people receive.
