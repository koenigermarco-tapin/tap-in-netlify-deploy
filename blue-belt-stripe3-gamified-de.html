üìä The Radical Candor Framework

Two axes. Four quadrants. One sweet spot.

The Two Dimensions

Care Personally: Do you genuinely care about this person as a human being?

Challenge Directly: Are you willing to tell them the uncomfortable truth?

üòä Ruinous Empathy

Care personally, but don't challenge. "You're doing great!" (when they're not). Feels kind, but hurts growth.

üéØ Radical Candor

üò§ Obnoxious Aggression

Challenge directly, but don't care personally. "This is terrible. Fix it." Honest, but damaging.

üì• Receiving Feedback Well

This is harder than giving it.

Your Default Reaction Will Fight You

When you hear critical feedback, your brain goes into defense mode. You want to explain, justify, minimize, or counterattack. This is natural. And it's also the enemy.

The ability to receive feedback without defensiveness is a superpower. Most people can't do it.

1. Listen First

Don't respond immediately. Let them finish. Take a breath. Your first instinct is usually to defend‚Äîdon't.

2. Seek to Understand

Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me an example?" or "What would you have liked to see instead?" You're trying to understand, not to rebut.

3. Thank Them

Even if the feedback stings, thank them for giving it. This encourages more feedback in the future‚Äîwhich is what you want.

4. Reflect Before Reacting

You don't have to agree immediately. Say "Let me think about this" and come back later with a considered response.

ü•ã The Tap as Feedback

In BJJ, the tap is the ultimate feedback: "That worked. I was caught." The best training partners don't get defensive when they tap. They ask: "What did I miss? Where did I go wrong?"

Every tap is a lesson. Every piece of feedback is the same.

üí°
The Real Test
How you respond to feedback determines whether you get more of it. Wenn du're defensive, people will stop being honest. Wenn du're grateful, they'll keep helping you improve.
