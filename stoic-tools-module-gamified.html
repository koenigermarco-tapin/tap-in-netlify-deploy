Part 1: Stoic Foundations

Stoicism begins with a single realization: some things are in your control, others are not. Freedom comes from clarity about which is which.

1. The Dichotomy of Control

What's in your control: your judgment, desire, aversion, and actions. What's not: your body, property, reputation, position.

‚≠ê The Core Dichotomy (Epictetus)
Some things are "up to us" (prohairesis) ‚Äî your will, effort, judgment. Others are "not up to us" ‚Äî body, health, wealth, others' opinions. Only the first are truly free.
üéØ Redirect Your Energy (Marcus Aurelius)
Stop fighting what you can't change. Pour your energy into your character, effort, and response. You can't control the storm, but you control how you navigate it.
"We cannot choose our external circumstances, but we can always choose how we respond to them." ‚Äî Epictetus
2. Impressions ‚Äî The Gatekeeper
üîç Separate Event From Judgment
Event: Someone criticizes your work. Impression 1: "I'm a failure." Impression 2: "They have a point, let me learn." Same event, different judgment. You control the judgment.
üí´ The Power of Perspective
Things are indifferent‚Äîthey have no inherent meaning. You assign meaning through your judgment. A setback is only bad if you judge it as bad. It could be opportunity.
3. Premeditatio Malorum ‚Äî Negative Visualization
üé≠ Rehearse Adversity
Spend 5 minutes imagining a challenge: losing a project, conflict, health scare. Not to spiral, but to visualize how you'd respond calmly. This builds resilience.
üôè Appreciate What You Have
Imagine losing what you value‚Äîhealth, relationships, job. Not to induce fear, but to wake you up to gratitude. This shifts you from taking things for granted to treasuring them.
üí° This Week: Stoic Foundations Practice
  • Daily: When stressed, apply Dichotomy of Control. Ask "Is this in my control?"
  • Once: Do a 5-minute negative visualization of one fear
  • Ongoing: Notice your impressions. Challenge one false judgment today

Part 2: Stoic Practices

Stoicism isn't theory‚Äîit's practice. These tools help you internalize wisdom and build resilience through daily action.

1. Journaling ‚Äî The Stoic Reflection
üåÖ Morning Priming
Start your day by reading one Stoic quote or principle. Write your intention: "Today, my focus is virtue/resilience/[specific challenge]." This primes your mindset before reactivity kicks in.
üåô Evening Reflection
Each night (5-10 min): What happened today? How did you respond? Where did you fall short of your values? Where did you excel? This isn't punishment‚Äîit's feedback.
üìä Three Columns Technique
Column 1: Event (what happened). Column 2: Impression (your automatic thought). Column 3: Better Response (rational, virtuous alternative). This externalizes and corrects distorted thinking.
"Waste no more time arguing what a good man should be. Be one." ‚Äî Marcus Aurelius
2. Amor Fati ‚Äî Love Your Fate
üõ§Ô∏è The Obstacle is the Way
Every obstacle is an opportunity to practice virtue. Rejection teaches resilience. Conflict teaches diplomacy. Failure teaches humility. The thing blocking your path becomes the path itself.
‚ö° Turn Setbacks Into Ammunition
A difficult colleague isn't your enemy‚Äîthey're training for patience. A rejection isn't failure‚Äîit's redirection. Reframe every setback as a tool for development.
3. The Four Cardinal Virtues
üéØ Wisdom, Courage, Justice, Temperance
1) Wisdom: sound judgment. 2) Courage: facing difficulty calmly. 3) Justice: fairness and duty. 4) Temperance: moderation and self-control. These are the only things that matter.
üéì Detach From Outcomes
Do your best with integrity. The outcome isn't fully in your control. But your effort and integrity always are. That's your only measure of success.

  • Create one "Three Columns" entry for a current frustration
  • Pick one virtue to focus on this week
  • Practice Amor Fati: reframe one setback as learning

Part 3: Stoic Application

Wisdom without practice is philosophy. These templates turn Stoic principles into daily action.

The Stoic Day Structure
üåÖ Morning (5 min)
Read one Stoic quote. Set your virtue for the day. Visualize challenges and how you'll respond.
‚òÄÔ∏è Midday (2 min)
Quick check-in. How am I doing on my virtue? Adjust if needed.
üåô Evening (5-10 min)
Reflect. What happened? How did I respond? Where did I fall short? Where did I excel? What will I do better tomorrow?
"The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way." ‚Äî Marcus Aurelius
Real-World Stoic Scenarios
üì¢ Public Failure/Criticism
Automatic: "I'm incompetent. Everyone thinks I'm bad." Stoic response: "I participated with my best thinking. The outcome isn't mine to control. I practice courage and humility by accepting feedback." Action: Ask for specific feedback. Learn. Move on.
‚öñÔ∏è Injustice/Unfairness
Automatic: "This is unfair! I'm being wronged!" Stoic check: Unfair things happen. Is anger productive? No. What IS in your control? How you respond. Options: Address it professionally, let it go and document, or build your reputation. Virtue: Justice, Courage, Wisdom.
üå™Ô∏è Uncertainty/Ambiguity
Automatic: "I don't know what's happening. I feel out of control." Stoic check: You never fully controlled your environment. Change is constant. What CAN you control? Your effort, attitude, adaptation. Response: Gather clarity where possible. Focus on what you CAN influence: skill, relationships, resilience.
Monthly Stoic Review
üóìÔ∏è Consolidate Your Progress
Questions: Which virtue did I practice best? Where did I lose my cool? Did I use the Dichotomy of Control? What obstacle taught me the most? Am I more resilient than last month?
